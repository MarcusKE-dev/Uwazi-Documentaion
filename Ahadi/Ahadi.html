AHADI

About this document. This guide explains what Ahadi is, why it exists, and how it works, for citizens, journalists, leaders and their offices, and anyone evaluating the platform. Ahadi is a civic technology initiative for Kenya, built on the principle that every promise a leader makes in public should remain a matter of public record. It is a sibling initiative to UWAZI, a separate proposed platform for public finance transparency.

Part 01 of 12

What Is Ahadi

A single, searchable national record of what Kenya's elected leaders have promised, and what has happened since.

Ahadi is a public accountability platform built around one simple idea: a promise made in public by an elected leader should stay on the public record until it is delivered, or until it is honestly marked as delayed, stalled, or abandoned. Not summarised from memory. Not left to whoever shouts loudest on social media at election time. Recorded once, with evidence, and updated as things actually change.

The platform covers every level of elected office in Kenya: the President and Deputy President nationally, Governors, Senators and Women Representatives at county level, Members of Parliament at constituency level, and Members of County Assembly at ward level. A citizen can search their own location, or a specific leader's name, and see every promise tied to that seat, in one place, with a dated history of what has happened since it was made.

For citizens

A record you can check

Enter your county, constituency or ward and see who represents you, what they have promised, and how far each promise has actually gone.

For journalists

A tool, not a replacement

Ahadi is built for journalists to publish through, not to publish instead of them. Every entry is created or reviewed by a verified newsroom account.

For leaders

A fair, factual record

Every promise is dated and sourced. Every leader has a standing right of reply. Nothing is published as an accusation; everything is published as a documented fact with its source attached.

What Ahadi is not

Ahadi does not rate leaders on popularity, ideology, or personal opinion. It does not accept anonymous, unverified claims for direct publication. It is not affiliated with any political party, and it does not accept political sponsorship. It records what was promised, what evidence exists, and what has happened since, and nothing else.

Part 02 of 12

Why This Matters

Kenya has a well documented trust gap between citizens and the leaders who make promises to them.

Public trust in Kenyan government institutions has been declining for years, not rising. According to the Edelman Trust Barometer's 2025 findings, only 38 percent of Kenyan adults say they trust government to do what is right, a further decline from the year before. Afrobarometer's research over the past decade shows trust in the ruling party falling from 61 percent to 38 percent, with confidence in the national electoral commission sitting even lower. A growing majority of Kenyans say they believe political leaders routinely mislead the public, a concern that has been rising since 2021.

This is not an abstract statistic. It shows up directly in how elections are fought and how leaders are judged once in office. Campaign promises are made loudly, at rallies and in manifestos, and then rarely revisited in any organised way once the campaign is over. By the next election cycle, most voters are working from memory, rumour, or whatever a candidate's opponents choose to remind them of, rather than an actual, verifiable record.

The constitutional basis

Article 35 of the Constitution of Kenya gives every citizen the right to information held by the state, and information held by another person where required for the exercise of any right. Article 232 requires public officers to be held accountable for administrative acts. Ahadi does not invent a new right; it makes an existing one practical, by putting the record in one searchable place instead of scattered across newspaper archives, parliamentary Hansards, and county gazette notices that most citizens will never think to look up.

Ahadi's premise is straightforward. If a promise was made in public, in front of a camera, a crowd, or a parliamentary record, then a citizen has a reasonable claim to know whether it happened. Making that record easy to find does not fix a trust gap by itself, but it removes one real barrier: the sheer difficulty of finding out.

Part 03 of 12

How Ahadi Works

The life of a promise, from the moment it is spoken to the day it is delivered, delayed, or dropped.

Every promise on Ahadi moves through the same disciplined process. Nothing skips a step, and nothing is published without a named journalist or editor taking responsibility for it.

1
A leader makes a promiseIn a speech, a rally, a parliamentary session, a TV interview, or an official address. It is a real, recorded, public moment, not a private conversation.
2
Evidence is capturedA video clip, an audio recording, an official transcript, a Hansard extract, or a news report, tied to a specific date and location.
3
It reaches a verified newsroom accountEither a journalist records it directly, or a citizen submits a tip with evidence, which sits in a review queue until a journalist looks at it.
4
A journalist or editor reviews itChecking the evidence, the date, the leader, and the location before anything goes live. Nothing a citizen submits publishes automatically.
5
The promise record is publishedWith its own permanent page: what was promised, the date, the deadline, the budget where known, the category, and the location it applies to.
6
The record is updated over time, never overwrittenAs a promise moves from promised to in progress, delayed, stalled, completed, or abandoned, each change is added as a new, dated, sourced entry.
7
The leader has a standing right of replyA leader or their office can have an official response recorded and shown clearly alongside the promise at any time.
8
It feeds the leader's accountability profileEvery published promise contributes automatically to that leader's overall record and score, recalculated as new information comes in.

Why a full history matters more than a single status

A promise that says only "completed" or "not completed" hides most of the story. Ahadi records the full sequence instead: when it was promised, when funding was approved, when work started, whether it stalled and why, and when it finally finished, or did not. Each entry in that sequence carries its own evidence and its own source. Reading the whole timeline tells a citizen far more than a single status label ever could.

Anatomy of a Promise Record

What information Ahadi actually records, and what counts as acceptable evidence.

Core fields recorded for every promise
FieldWhat it captures
Leader & positionWho made the promise, and the specific office they held at the time (a promise stays attached to the term it was made under, even after an election).
Date madeThe exact date the promise was spoken or published, not the date it was entered into the system.
DeadlineAny stated completion date, where the leader gave one. Left blank rather than guessed when none was given.
BudgetThe stated cost of the promise, where disclosed. Left at zero rather than estimated when no figure was given publicly.
CategoryInfrastructure, health, education, water, security, environment, economy, governance, legislation, or youth and ICT.
LocationNational, county, constituency, or ward, tied to the actual administrative area the promise applies to.
Status & progressThe current state of the promise, and a percentage estimate of how far it has progressed.
EvidenceOne or more sourced items: video, audio, an official document, a photograph, or a news report, attached to every entry in the timeline.
Reported byThe named newsroom or journalist account responsible for that entry, visible on the public record.
Right of replyAn optional, clearly labelled response from the leader or their office, shown separately from the newsroom's own reporting.

Status lifecycle

Every promise sits in exactly one of six states at any given time. The label is chosen by a journalist, backed by the evidence attached to that update.

StatusWhat it means
PromisedThe promise has been made and recorded. No visible action has been confirmed yet.
In ProgressConfirmed activity is under way: funding approved, a tender awarded, construction started, or similar.
DelayedWork is behind its stated timeline, but has not stopped entirely.
StalledWork has stopped, with no confirmed activity for a meaningful period, and no completion in sight.
CompletedThe promise has been fulfilled, confirmed by evidence such as a completion certificate, site visit, or official record.
AbandonedThere is clear evidence the promise has been dropped: funds redirected, the project cancelled, or no explanation given after an extended period of inaction.
On evidence standards

Acceptable evidence includes video or audio of the promise itself, official transcripts, parliamentary Hansard extracts, gazette notices, budget and procurement documents, dated site photographs, and reporting from established news outlets. A citizen's tip alone is never sufficient for publication; it becomes part of the record only once a journalist has reviewed it against evidence of this kind.

A deliberately honest approach to who is listed

Every seat above exists in Ahadi's directory, searchable by name and location, whether or not a verified leader record has been attached to it yet. Where a leader's identity has been checked against multiple independent sources, their name is shown. Where it has not yet been verified, the seat is shown clearly as pending verification rather than filled with a guessed or unconfirmed name.

Why this matters

A platform that puts a name to a real person's office needs to be right, not just complete. Filling in a plausible sounding name for a seat that has not actually been verified would risk a citizen mistaking an unverified guess for their real representative. Ahadi treats an honest "pending verification" as strictly better than a confident wrong answer.

As of this document's publication, the President, the Deputy President, three county Governors, and six constituency MPs have been verified against multiple independent sources and are shown by name. Every other seat is visible in the directory and awaits verification as the newsroom grows. See Part 11 for the current, honest count.

Accountability Scores & Comparison

A single, explainable number, built from the promise record itself, not from opinion polling.

Once a leader has published promises on record, Ahadi calculates an accountability score out of 100, built from four measurable components. Nothing in the formula is a judgement call by an editor; it is a calculation over the leader's own recorded history.

Delivery
Transparency
Budget efficiency
Average progress

Illustrative example only, not a real leader's score.

Delivery

The share of a leader's promises that reached "completed," reduced when promises are marked stalled or abandoned.

Transparency

How much verifiable evidence backs each of a leader's promises on average. More sourced updates, higher transparency.

Budget efficiency

Reduced when promises run past their deadline or stall outright, since delay and inaction both carry a real cost.

Average progress

Where a leader's promises currently stand overall, including the ones still under way.

A leader with no published promises yet shows no score at all, rather than a default or an assumed zero. A score only exists once there is a real record to calculate it from.

Comparing leaders

Any two leaders can be compared directly: promises made, completed, delayed or stalled, completion rate, and overall score, side by side on the same scale. This is designed for the moment that matters most, when a voter is deciding between an incumbent and a challenger, or comparing their own representative against a neighbouring seat.

Part 07 of 12

The Newsroom Model

Journalists are the gatekeepers on Ahadi. There is no automated system that publishes claims on its own.

This is a deliberate design choice, not a limitation waiting to be automated away. Extracting a "promise" reliably from a speech, in English, Kiswahili, Sheng, or a mix of all three, is a genuinely hard problem, and getting it wrong at scale would put false claims into a record meant to be trusted. Ahadi keeps a person accountable for every entry instead.

RoleCan do
JournalistCreate new promise records, log dated timeline updates with evidence, record a leader's right of reply.
EditorEverything a journalist can do, plus reviewing the citizen report queue, and closing a leader's term (resignation, death, impeachment, election loss) with a successor recorded.
AdminFull editorial access, including account and platform-level administration.

Every action is logged

Ahadi keeps an audit trail of every editorial action: who did it, exactly when, and what changed. This is not a feature that can be turned off. It exists so that if a claim is ever disputed, there is a clear, timestamped record of who published it and why, and so that the platform's own conduct is as accountable as the leaders it tracks.

Access is authenticated, not open

Newsroom accounts require a verified login before any record can be created or changed. Passwords are never stored in plain text. Every write action, not just login, is checked against the account's role before it is allowed to happen.

Part 09 of 12

Technology & Security

Built to be verifiable and hard to quietly tamper with, in plain terms first, then in technical detail.

Ahadi is built as two separate pieces: a public website anyone can browse, and a database and programming interface behind it that only authenticated newsroom accounts can write to. Nothing on the public side can change the record directly. Every change goes through the newsroom system, and every change is logged.

Public interfaceSearchable directories, leader profiles, promise timelines, comparison tool
Newsroom panelAuthenticated access for journalists, editors and admins only
Application programming interfaceEvery read and write action passes through here, nothing bypasses it
Authentication layerHashed passwords, session tokens, role checks on every write
DatabaseLeaders, offices, promises, timeline updates and evidence, kept as separate, linked records
Audit logA permanent, append-only record of every editorial action taken on the platform

Why a leader's office is separate from the person holding it

An MP for a given constituency and the person who happens to hold that seat today are treated as two different things in the data. When a leader resigns, dies in office, is removed, or loses an election, their term is closed with a reason and a date, and a successor is attached to the office going forward. The outgoing leader's full history stays exactly as it was. Nothing about a resignation or an election result requires rewriting the record.

Technical summary

A React front end serves the public site. A Node.js and Express backend exposes a REST API. Data is held in a relational database with leaders, offices, and terms modelled as separate, linked tables, so that election results and resignations never overwrite history. Authentication uses hashed passwords and signed session tokens, with role checks enforced on every write endpoint, not just at login. Every promise update carries its own evidence records rather than a single overwritable status field. The architecture is built to scale from a single-server deployment toward a larger database with read replicas and caching as usage grows, without a redesign.

Part 10 of 12

Trust, Neutrality & Editorial Standards

The single biggest risk to a platform like this is being seen, correctly or not, as favouring one side.

Ahadi is built around a small set of standards that are not optional and not adjusted case by case.

No political sponsorship

Ahadi does not accept funding or sponsorship from political parties, candidates, or their affiliated organisations.

Every claim is sourced

No promise, and no status change, is published without dated, attributable evidence behind it.

Corrections, not deletions

If a published entry turns out to be wrong, it is corrected with a new, dated entry explaining the correction, not silently edited or removed.

A standing right of reply

Any leader or their office can have an official response recorded and shown clearly alongside any promise concerning them, at any time.

Consistent standards across every party

The same evidence bar, the same status definitions, and the same review process apply regardless of party, region, or office.

Applies equally to Ahadi itself

Every editorial action is logged and attributable. The platform holds its own conduct to the same transparency standard it asks of leaders.

A citizen does not have to trust Ahadi's judgement about a leader. They only have to trust that the evidence shown is real and the timeline is accurate, and check it themselves if they want to.

Why so much is marked pending, on purpose

It would have been straightforward to fill every seat with a plausible name and every promise with a plausible story. Ahadi does not do that. Directory structure without a confirmed name is honest. A confident, unverified guess is not, and once trust in a specific record breaks, it is hard to win back trust in the rest of the platform.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions from citizens, leaders, and newsroom partners.

Is Ahadi affiliated with any political party or the government?

No. Ahadi is an independent civic technology initiative. It does not accept political sponsorship, and the same standards apply to every leader regardless of party or office.

Can a leader or their team get a promise removed from the record?

No. A published promise stays on the record. If a leader disputes how it is described, the correct route is a recorded right of reply, or evidence supporting a correction, which is added as a new, dated entry rather than a deletion.

What happens when a leader resigns, dies in office, or loses an election?

Their term is closed with a stated reason and date, and a successor is recorded going forward if one exists. The outgoing leader's full promise history remains exactly as it was, unchanged and still searchable.

Is my report to the newsroom anonymous?

Contact information is optional when reporting a tip. If you leave it, it is used only so a journalist can follow up on your report, and it is not published.

How is a leader's name verified before it is published?

Against multiple independent sources: official government or parliamentary records, and at least one established news outlet. If that bar is not met, the seat is shown as pending verification instead of a name.

Does Ahadi use artificial intelligence to detect or write promises?

No. Every promise record and every timeline update is created or reviewed by a named journalist or editor. This is a deliberate design decision, not a temporary limitation.

Who can see the audit log?

Newsroom editors and admins can see the full audit log of editorial actions on the platform, as part of holding Ahadi's own process to the same standard it asks of the leaders it tracks.

Conclusion

A Debt That Should Stay on the Record

"Ahadi ni deni," a promise is a debt, is a common Swahili saying, and it is the idea the platform is named for. A debt does not disappear because it is inconvenient to remember. Ahadi exists to make sure a public promise does not either: recorded once, with evidence, held open until it is honestly resolved, and available to any citizen who wants to check.

Ahadi is not finished. Most of Kenya's elected seats still show pending verification rather than a confirmed name, and the platform's real value grows with every journalist who joins and every promise that gets recorded. That is by design. A national accountability record built honestly, one verified entry at a time, is worth more than one built quickly and wrong.

About Ahadi

Ahadi is a civic technology initiative for Kenya, built on the principle that journalists, not automated systems, should stand behind every claim published about a public leader. It is a sibling project to UWAZI, a separate proposed platform focused on public finance transparency, sharing the same underlying commitment to making public information easy to find and hard to quietly bury.
